# Email Authoring Guidance Notes

Date: 15 June 2026
Use case: First-contact and early follow-up emails for UK target accounts.

## 1. Core Rules

- Do not start sentences with `I` or `I'm` in first-contact emails.
- Open with the recipient, the company context, or the business reason for writing.
- Keep first-contact emails short enough to read comfortably on a phone.
- Lead with a review, relevance check, or practical business angle rather than a product pitch.
- Keep the tone professional, calm, and low-pressure.

## 2. Salutation Preference

- Prefer `Good morning Ms [Surname],` or `Good afternoon Ms [Surname],` for a first email to a named senior contact.
- Use `Ms` unless the recipient's preferred title is explicitly known from a reliable source.
- Do not guess between `Mrs` and `Miss`.
- Prefer surname-based address on first contact rather than first-name familiarity.
- If the contact replies using first-name tone, mirror that in later exchanges if appropriate.

## 3. Opening Sentence Guidance

Preferred opening styles:

- Reference company scale, complexity, or operating context.
- Reference local relevance briefly where it is genuinely helpful.
- Reference the business issue being explored.

Examples:

- `Given [Company]'s scale and supplier complexity, there may be value in a short review of supplier payment visibility and finance-process friction.`
- `With [Company]'s Southampton presence and broad supplier environment, a short review of payment visibility and working capital opportunity may be worthwhile.`
- `Because [Company] appears to manage a complex supplier and operational environment, a short review of payment visibility and AP friction may be timely.`

Avoid:

- `I work with Corporate LinX on UK growth.`
- `I'm reaching out because...`
- Any sentence that starts with the sender rather than the recipient, the company, or the business issue.

## 4. Structure For First Emails

Recommended structure:

1. Salutation using `Good morning/afternoon Ms [Surname],`
2. One sentence on why the company was singled out
3. One short sentence on the review focus
4. One short sentence on what Corporate LinX typically helps improve
5. One low-pressure call to action

## 5. Tone Preferences

- Sound commercially aware rather than sales-led.
- Use finance and operations language, not marketing language.
- Keep local references brief and natural.
- Ask whether a short conversation would be worthwhile, or easy to rule out.
- Make it easy for the recipient to redirect if the topic sits elsewhere.

## 6. Phrases To Prefer

- `there may be value in a short review`
- `would it be worth a short conversation`
- `supplier payment visibility`
- `finance-process friction`
- `working capital opportunity`
- `without forcing major systems change`
- `or to rule it out quickly`

## 7. Phrases To Avoid

- `just touching base`
- `circle back`
- `game changer`
- `transformational platform`
- `book a demo`
- `quick win` unless clearly evidenced
- overfamiliar greetings on first contact

## 8. Default First-Contact Pattern

Subject:

`Possible supplier payment and working capital review at [Company]`

Body:

`Good morning/afternoon Ms [Surname],`

`Given [Company]'s scale, supplier complexity, and [local/operating context if useful], there may be value in a short review focused on supplier payment visibility, finance-process friction, and any working capital opportunity linked to that.`

`Corporate LinX typically helps larger organisations improve visibility over invoice and payment status, reduce supplier noise, and identify practical process or cash benefits without forcing major systems change.`

`Would a short conversation be worth having to see whether that would be relevant for [Company], or to rule it out quickly?`

`Best regards,`

`Paul Ward`

## 9. Working Rule

If a draft contains a sentence that opens with `I` or `I'm`, rewrite it before sending.
